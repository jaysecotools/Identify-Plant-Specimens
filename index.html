Welcome to AHCPCM303: Identify Plant Specimens

Overview of Plant Identification

Welcome to the fascinating world of plant identification! This unit will equip you with the essential skills to accurately identify plant specimens using a range of techniques and resources. Whether you're working in conservation, land management, horticulture, or environmental consulting, the ability to correctly identify plants is fundamental to your role.

Why Plant Identification Matters in Tasmania

Tasmania is home to over 2,300 native vascular plant species, with approximately 580 species (25%) being endemic to the state. From the ancient Gondwanan rainforests to the unique alpine ecosystems, accurate identification is crucial for conservation, weed management, and ecological restoration.

Tasmanian Flora Facts Flashcards

How many native vascular plant species occur in Tasmania?

Approximately 2,300 native vascular plant species, with around 580 species (25%) being endemic to Tasmania.

What is Tasmania's floral emblem?

The Tasmanian Blue Gum (Eucalyptus globulus) was proclaimed as the state's floral emblem in 1962.

Taxonomic Hierarchy with Tasmanian Examples

The taxonomic hierarchy provides a structured way to classify and name plants based on their evolutionary relationships. From broadest to most specific, the levels are:

Taxonomic hierarchy levels with Tasmanian examples
Rank Description Tasmanian Example
Domain Eukarya (all organisms with membrane-bound nuclei) All Tasmanian plants
Kingdom Plantae (photosynthetic, multicellular) All Tasmanian native plants
Division (Phylum) Major evolutionary lineage (e.g., Angiosperms, Gymnosperms) Magnoliophyta (flowering plants)
Class Within division, based on major structural features Magnoliopsida (dicots) or Liliopsida (monocots)
Order Group of related families Myrtales (includes Myrtaceae family)
Family Group of related genera Myrtaceae (eucalypts, tea-trees)
Genus Group of closely related species Eucalyptus
Species Basic unit of classification, capable of interbreeding Eucalyptus globulus (Tasmanian Blue Gum)

Binomial Nomenclature Rules with 20+ Tasmanian Examples

Binomial nomenclature, developed by Carl Linnaeus, gives each species a unique two-part scientific name:

  • Genus (capitalized) + species epithet (lowercase), both italicized or underlined
  • When handwritten, underline separately: Eucalyptus globulus
  • Always include the author citation in formal contexts (e.g., Eucalyptus globulus Labill.)

Leaf Morphology Flashcards

Lanceolate

Lance-shaped, broader at the base and tapering to the tip. Example: Adult Eucalyptus globulus leaves.

Revolute

Leaf margins rolled under. Example: Richea pandanifolia leaves, an adaptation to reduce water loss in alpine environments.

Phyllode

A flattened petiole that functions as a leaf. Example: Acacia melanoxylon (Blackwood) has phyllodes instead of true leaves.

Bipinnate

Twice-divided pinnate leaves. Example: Acacia dealbata (Silver Wattle) has bipinnate leaves.

Tasmanian Examples of Specialized Stems and Roots

Tasmanian Plant Adaptations
  • Lignotubers: Many eucalypts (e.g., Eucalyptus coccifera) have lignotubers that allow regeneration after fire.
  • Proteoid roots: Telopea truncata and other Proteaceae produce dense cluster roots to access phosphorus in poor soils.
  • Adventitious roots: Dicksonia antarctica (tree fern) produces a dense mat of roots along its trunk.
  • Rhizomes: Pteridium esculentum (bracken fern) spreads via extensive underground rhizomes.
  • Cladodes: Phyllocladus aspleniifolius (celery-top pine) uses flattened stems (cladodes) for photosynthesis.

Section 6: Using Identification Keys

Dichotomous Keys: Principles and Practice

Dichotomous keys are essential tools for plant identification, presenting a series of paired choices that lead the user to the correct species name. Understanding how to construct and use these keys is fundamental to botanical work.

Key Terminology
  • Dichotomous: Divided into two parts - each couplet presents two contrasting statements
  • Couplet: A pair of contrasting descriptions
  • Lead: One of the two options in a couplet
  • Indented key: Subordinate leads are indented to show hierarchy
  • Bracketed key: Leads are numbered and direct to subsequent couplets
Comparison of different key types
Key Type Description Advantages Disadvantages
Indented (Yoked) Subsequent leads indented under previous choices Visually clear hierarchy, easy to see relationships Can become messy with long keys, requires careful formatting
Bracketed (Parallel) Each couplet numbered, with directions to next number Compact, efficient for long keys Requires flipping pages, less visual
Single-access Traditional linear keys, one path to identification Systematic, proven method Cannot skip steps, one wrong choice leads to error
Multi-access Interactive keys using any combination of characters Flexible, can use available characters, faster Requires database or software, may need more characters

Creating Your Own Keys

Practice constructing dichotomous keys for Tasmanian plant groups. Follow these principles:

Key Construction Activity
  1. Choose contrasting characteristics: Use stable, observable features (leaf arrangement, flower parts, fruit type)
  2. Start with obvious features: Begin with characteristics that divide the group into two clear halves
  3. Use consistent terminology: Apply botanical terms correctly and consistently
  4. Avoid overlapping choices: Ensure each couplet presents mutually exclusive options
  5. Test your key: Verify it works with known specimens

Section 7: Tasmanian Threatened Species

Conservation Legislation Overview

Tasmania's threatened flora is protected under both Commonwealth and State legislation. Understanding these legal frameworks is essential for conservation work.

Overview of conservation legislation in Tasmania
Legislation Jurisdiction Categories Key Features
EPBC Act 1999 Commonwealth Critically Endangered, Endangered, Vulnerable, Conservation Dependent National environment law, triggers for assessment and approval
Threatened Species Protection Act 1995 Tasmania Extinct, Extinct in Wild, Critically Endangered, Endangered, Vulnerable, Rare State-level listing, listing statements, recovery plans
Nature Conservation Act 2002 Tasmania Reserved, Protected, Protected Wildlife General conservation framework

EPBC Act Listing Categories

  • Critically Endangered: Facing an extremely high risk of extinction in the wild in the immediate future
  • Endangered: Facing a very high risk of extinction in the wild in the near future
  • Vulnerable: Facing a high risk of extinction in the wild in the medium-term future
  • Conservation Dependent: The focus of a specific conservation program, without which the species would become vulnerable

Tasmanian TSP Act 1995 Categories

  • Extinct: No reasonable doubt that the last individual has died
  • Extinct in the Wild: Known only to survive in cultivation
  • Critically Endangered: Extremely high risk of extinction
  • Endangered: Very high risk of extinction
  • Vulnerable: High risk of extinction
  • Rare: Small population that is not currently endangered but is at risk

Section 8: Herbarium Techniques

Specimen Collection Protocols

Proper collection and preservation of plant specimens is essential for scientific documentation, verification, and long-term reference.

Collection Ethics and Permits
  • Obtain necessary permits before collecting (NRE, Parks Tasmania, landholder permission)
  • Collect only what is needed - minimal impact principle
  • Never collect threatened species without specific permits
  • Document location, habitat, and associated species
  • Photograph plants in situ before collecting

Pressing and Drying Techniques

Step-by-Step Pressing Guide
  1. Arrange specimen: Position to show both surfaces of leaves, flowers, and fruits. Include a small portion of stem showing leaf arrangement.
  2. Fold or cut: If specimen is too long, fold in a 'V' or 'N' shape, or cut into sections.
  3. Separate with paper: Place specimen between sheets of absorbent paper (newsprint or blotting paper).
  4. Add corrugated cardboard: Between every 1-2 specimens to allow air circulation.
  5. Tighten press: Apply firm, even pressure with straps.
  6. Dry: Change blotting paper daily for first few days, then every 2-3 days until dry (usually 1-3 weeks).
  7. Heat drying: For rapid drying, use a plant drier with gentle heat (35-40C).

Digital Herbarium Records

Modern herbaria maintain digital databases with high-resolution images and searchable records. Key data fields include:

  • Images: Links to specimen images, habitat photos

Question 2.1
Describe the key differences between simple and compound leaves, with Tasmanian examples of each.
Simple leaves have a single blade (e.g., Eucalyptus globulus). Compound leaves have multiple leaflets (e.g., Polystichum proliferum, Acacia dealbata).
Question 2.2
List three Tasmanian gymnosperms and their conservation status under the EPBC Act.
1. Athrotaxis selaginoides - Vulnerable, 2. Athrotaxis cupressoides - Vulnerable, 3. Athrotaxis laxifolia - Vulnerable

What are phyllodes and which Tasmanian genus commonly exhibits them?
Phyllodes are flattened petioles that function as leaves. Acacia (e.g., Acacia melanoxylon) commonly exhibits phyllodes.
Question 2.4
Name four different bark types found in Tasmanian eucalypts and provide an example species for each.

Question 2.5
Describe the correct procedure for pressing a plant specimen for herbarium storage.
Arrange specimen to show key features, place between absorbent paper, add corrugated cardboard for air flow, apply even pressure in press, change blotting paper regularly until dry (1-3 weeks).

Glossary of Botanical Terms

Index

A

Achene

A small, dry, indehiscent fruit containing a single seed, with the seed attached to the fruit wall at only one point.

Example: Fruits of Bedfordia salicina (Asteraceae)

Acicular

Needle-shaped, slender and pointed.

Example: Leaves of Microcachrys tetragona

Actinomorphic

Radially symmetrical flowers that can be divided into equal halves along multiple planes.

Example: Flowers of Epacris impressa

Acuminate

Tapering gradually to a pointed tip with concave sides.

Example: Leaf tips of some Eucalyptus species

Adventitious

Roots that develop from stems or leaves rather than from the primary root system.

Example: Roots on the trunk of Dicksonia antarctica

Alternate

Leaf arrangement with one leaf per node, arranged alternately along the stem.

Example: Adult leaves of Eucalyptus globulus

Amplexicaul

Leaf base that clasps the stem.

Example: Leaves of some Eucalyptus species

Androecium

The male reproductive structures of a flower, consisting of stamens.

Example: Stamens of Eucalyptus globulus

Angiosperm

Flowering plants that produce seeds enclosed within an ovary.

Example: Most Tasmanian plants, including Eucalyptus, Nothofagus

Anther

The pollen-bearing part of a stamen.

Example: Yellow anthers of Acacia flowers

Aril

A fleshy, often colorful structure that covers or attaches to a seed.

Example: Red arils of Atherosperma moschatum

B

Berry

A fleshy, indehiscent fruit with seeds embedded in the flesh.

Example: Fruits of Tasmannia lanceolata

Bipinnate

Twice-divided pinnate leaves, where leaflets are further divided into smaller leaflets.

Example: Leaves of Acacia dealbata

Bract

A modified leaf associated with a flower or inflorescence, often different in appearance from normal leaves.

Example: Showy bracts of Telopea truncata

Flared roots at the base of a tree trunk for support.

Example: Nothofagus cunninghamii in rainforests

The outer whorl of a flower, composed of sepals.

Example: Green sepals of Epacris impressa

Capsule

A dry, dehiscent fruit that splits open when ripe, derived from a compound ovary.

Example: Fruits of Eucalyptus and Epacris

Carpel

The female reproductive unit of a flower, consisting of stigma, style, and ovary.

Example: Single carpel in Nothofagus flowers

Catkin

A slender, pendulous spike of unisexual flowers.

Example: Male flowers of Nothofagus cunninghamii

Cauliflory

Flowers and fruits that develop directly from the trunk or branches.

Example: Occasional in Acacia melanoxylon

Cladode

A flattened stem that functions as a leaf.

Example: Phyllocladus aspleniifolius

Compound leaf

A leaf divided into multiple leaflets.

Example: Fern fronds, Acacia dealbata

Cordate

Heart-shaped leaf with the notch at the base.

Example: Leaves of Anodopetalum biglandulosum

Corm

A solid, swollen underground stem base used for storage.

Example: Some orchids

Corolla

The inner whorl of a flower, composed of petals.

Example: Red petals of Telopea truncata

Crenate

Leaf margin with rounded teeth.

Example: Leaves of Bedfordia salicina

Critically Endangered

Facing an extremely high risk of extinction in the wild in the immediate future.

Example: Epacris stuartii, Caladenia tonellii

D

Decurrent

Leaf base that extends downward along the stem as wings.

Example: Some Eucalyptus species

Dehiscent

A fruit that splits open at maturity to release seeds.

Triangular leaf shape.

Divided into two parts; in keys, presenting two contrasting choices at each step.

Example: Dichotomous keys for plant identification

Angiosperms with two seed leaves (cotyledons), usually with net-veined leaves and flower parts in multiples of four or five.

A fleshy fruit with a hard, stony endocarp surrounding the seed.

Example: Fruits of Pomaderris apetala

Leaf tip with a shallow notch.

Native to and restricted to a particular geographic area.

A plant that grows on another plant without being parasitic.
